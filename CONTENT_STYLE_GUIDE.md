# Content Style Guide

- Write to a capable adult beginner in warm, direct language.
- Aim for roughly grade 8–10 readability; prefer short sentences and concrete verbs.
- Define a financial term before or when it first appears.
- Use the Maple Street family story to clarify a concept, not to manufacture urgency.
- Repeat hard ideas in a second form when useful. Alternate explanations must be prewritten.
- Ask one thing at a time. Every practice and quiz choice receives explanatory feedback.
- Treat uncertainty and mistakes as normal parts of learning.
- Never use hype, fear, shame, profit celebrations, fake scarcity, or trading-game language.
- Make the experience compelling through curiosity, competence, story continuity, varied practice, and visible mastery—not addiction, streak anxiety, random rewards, or loss aversion.
- Ask for a meaningful learner action after a complete idea, not after every paragraph. Vary prediction, sorting, modeling, routing, explanation, and recall.
- Preserve an introductory college-course hierarchy: learning outcome, concept explanation, worked example, application, and summary.
- Use the recurring story as a case study. Never let story, points, rewards, or interface decoration replace direct teaching.
- Say “may,” “can,” and “generally” where outcomes vary. Never imply that investing is risk-free.
- Keep examples neutral and fictional. Do not assume income, savings, family structure, or prior experience.
